# Question to Audience

## What this angle is

A specific question the user genuinely wants answered. Not rhetorical. Not an engagement-bait question stapled onto another post. The question IS the post — and it's narrow enough that readers can answer with a real personal moment, not a generic opinion.

The reader's reaction is to type an actual answer in the comments. Question to Audience works because most "questions" on social media are bait ("agree?"). A question that's genuinely curious, narrow enough to invite a specific answer, and broad enough to have many possible answers gets long reply threads — which is what the algorithm rewards on Threads.

## Signal in the dump

The dump contains a Question to Audience if the user:
- Wonders something out loud and wants to know what others think
- Asks "is it just me" type questions where they suspect the answer is no
- Has a question that's been sitting in their head with no resolution
- Wants to compare their experience to others' on something specific

Phrases to look for: "is it just me?", "does anyone else", "I've been wondering", "I want to know", "what do people do when", "drop your".

## Structure

A Question to Audience is short. The question must do all the work, so the question itself has to be sharp.

**Variant A — Bare question (1 sentence)**

Just the question. Nothing else. Used when the question is interesting enough on its own.

```
[The question — narrow, specific, real]
```

**Variant B — One-line setup + question (2-3 sentences)**

A short setup that establishes context or stakes, then the question. The setup must be one line max — longer than that and the post becomes about the user, not the audience.

```
[One line of setup or context]

[The question]
```

**Variant C — Question with anti-bait fence (2-4 sentences)**

The question, plus a list of obvious answers the user is telling readers *not* to give. This forces non-generic responses and signals the user is genuinely curious.

```
[The question]

[The fence — "not X, not Y, not Z" — narrowing what counts as an answer]
```

## Examples that work

**Bare question:**

> Does anyone else feel like grocery shopping has become a $200 minimum lately?

> What would you regret on your deathbed?

> Men if you could please chime in. What's the guy version of flowers? Like what just-because gift can make you feel appreciated?

**Setup + question:**

> Neurodivergent question — do you need some kind of background noise to focus, but the second it's the wrong kind your entire brain short circuits?

> The "post consistently" advice ignores something. Would you rather post every day with average content, or twice a week when you have something real to say?

**Question with anti-bait fence:**

> Drop your most UNHINGED woo-woo habit that actually changed your life. Not meditation. Not journaling. Not tarot. Not crystals. I mean extra weird. Like "I can't tell my coworkers" weird.

> Name a movie character that technically wasn't a villain but you consider them to be one. I'll start: [example].

## Banned openings

The question should arrive immediately. Apologetic or bait-y openings kill the angle.

- "Quick question:"
- "Random thought but…"
- "Is this just me, or…" (used as filler — fine if it's the actual question)
- "Hot take but I want to hear yours:"
- "Curious to hear what you think about…"
- "What are your thoughts on…"

Either ask the question directly or give one line of context, then the question. Anything else is wasted.

## Banned closings

The question ends the post. Adding more weakens it.

- "Comment below ⬇️"
- "I'll wait."
- "Drop your answer 👇"
- "I genuinely want to know"
- A second question (this dilutes both)
- The user's own answer pre-filled at the end

The reader knows where to comment. Telling them is condescending and reads as bait.

## Anti-patterns

- **Question with an obvious answer.** "Is hard work important?" "Do you want to be successful?" — no real range of answers. Question to Audience requires actual variation in plausible answers.
- **Too broad to answer specifically.** "What do you think about life?" — the reader can't even start. The question has to be narrow enough that a specific answer fits in one comment.
- **Rhetorical question.** "Why do we even bother?" — looks like a question, isn't one. The user isn't actually asking.
- **Question stapled onto another post.** A 200-word essay followed by "what do you think?" doesn't make this angle. The question must BE the post.
- **Question that's actually a Hot Take in disguise.** "Why do people still post on Instagram in 2026?" — implies a position. Either commit to the Hot Take or ask a real question.
- **Too many qualifiers.** "I'm just curious, no judgement, just wondering, what do you all think about…" — the throat-clearing kills the question. Ask flat.
- **Question with engagement-bait format.** "Like for X, comment for Y" — the angle isn't a poll. Ask one question that invites real answers.

## Self-check before saving

Read the question alone and ask: **could a stranger answer this with a specific personal moment?** If the only answers are "yes/no" or "I agree/disagree" — the question is too closed. If the only answers are essays — the question is too broad. The sweet spot is a question that invites one concrete thing from the reader's own life.
