# Observation

## What this angle is

The user noticed a pattern — in how people behave, how a tool works, how an industry operates, how something plays out repeatedly. Not a personal story. Not an opinion. A spotted regularity, named.

The reader's reaction is "yes, I see this too" or "wait, is that actually true?". Observation works because most people register patterns subconsciously without articulating them; when someone names the pattern out loud, recognition hits. Different from Hot Take in that it isn't pushing against consensus — it's pointing at something nobody bothered to put into words.

## Signal in the dump

The dump contains an Observation if the user:
- Says they noticed something — about people, tools, the industry, a behavior
- Names a pattern they've seen play out multiple times
- Points at a contradiction in how something is supposed to work vs how it actually works
- Comments on what *everyone seems to* do or what *nobody* talks about

Phrases to look for: "I noticed", "have you noticed", "everyone seems to", "weird that", "every time I", "the pattern is", "people always", "nobody mentions".

## Structure

An Observation names the pattern in the first line and either stops or gives one beat of evidence/twist.

**Variant A — Bare pattern (1-2 sentences)**

State the pattern. Done. Used when the pattern is so recognizable on its own that it doesn't need defense.

```
[The pattern, named cleanly — no setup]
```

**Variant B — Pattern + evidence (2-4 sentences)**

The pattern, then one specific instance or contrast that makes it concrete.

```
[The pattern named]

[One instance, contrast, or twist that confirms it]
```

**Variant C — Two-tier observation (3-5 sentences)**

The surface-level thing first, then the underlying pattern. Used when the contrast between what people *say* and what *is* is the point.

```
[The visible behavior or claim]

[The underlying pattern that contradicts it]
```

## Examples that work

**Bare pattern:**

> One thing I've noticed is that every woman I know over 40 has absolutely had it.

> No one at your funeral is going to say, "She was so responsive on Slack."

> The smartest people do nothing for hours everyday.

**Pattern + evidence:**

> Society: "Just meal prep!"
> ADHD Brain: *buys ingredients*
> Dopamine: *leaves chat*
> Executive Function: *error 404*
> Vegetables: *die in fridge*
> Bank Account: *cries in takeout*

> BRUTAL TRUTH: Women pretending not to see men staring at them is an essential survival skill.

**Two-tier observation:**

> Instagram: "we don't know why people aren't posting on the app anymore :("
> Also Instagram: [shows your posts to literally no one]

> Instagram is for pretending you're fine. LinkedIn is for pretending you have it together. Threads is where you admit you cried in the shower again.

> The fake urgency created in corporate life for absolutely no reason is one of the worst things humans have invented.

## Banned openings

The observation should land on the pattern itself, not introduce that one is coming.

- "Has anyone else noticed that…"
- "Am I the only one who thinks…"
- "I've been thinking about how…"
- "Random thought:"
- "Just noticed:"
- "Quick observation:"

Drop the framing. The pattern itself is the opening.

## Banned closings

Observations land on the named pattern. Adding more weakens it.

- "Just me?"
- "Anyone relate?"
- "What do you think?"
- A long explanation of why the pattern exists
- A solution to the pattern

The reader will either recognize the pattern or won't. Explanation kills the recognition.

## Anti-patterns

- **Pattern that's actually an opinion.** "I've noticed that loud people are insecure" — this is a Hot Take wearing observation clothes. The Observation angle requires the pattern to be visible to others, not a personal verdict.
- **Pattern that's already saturated.** "Everyone's always on their phones" / "Nobody listens anymore" — these have been said a thousand times. The angle requires a pattern that hasn't been named to death.
- **Pattern with no specific behavior.** "People are just weird these days." Too vague to recognize. The Observation needs a concrete pattern the reader can picture.
- **Single-instance dressed as a pattern.** "I had one bad customer service experience, therefore X is broken." A pattern requires multiple instances or a recognizable system, not one data point.
- **Observation followed by self-promotion.** "I noticed nobody talks about this — that's why I built [thing]." The angle dies the moment the post starts selling.
- **Pattern + moral lesson.** Naming a pattern is enough. The moment the post tells the reader what to do about it, the observation flattens into advice.

## Self-check before saving

Read the draft and ask: **would a stranger nod and say "yeah, I see this"?** If you can't picture a stranger recognizing the pattern, it's either too niche or the user is the only person who's seen it — which makes it a Specific Story, not an Observation.
