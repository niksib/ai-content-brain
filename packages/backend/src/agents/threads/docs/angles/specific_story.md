# Specific Story

## What this angle is

A real moment narrated with concrete details — time, place, what was said, who said it. Not a parable. Not a lesson dressed up as a story. The scene itself is the post.

The reader gets pulled in by specifics they can picture. The reaction is "same thing happened to me", "wait, what happened next?", or "this is exactly how it goes". A Specific Story works because real detail is rare on the internet — most posts are summary or opinion. A scene with names, dialogue, and timing reads as true.

## Signal in the dump

The dump contains a Specific Story if the user:
- Describes a moment that happened — not a general experience
- Names specific people, places, times, or words spoken
- Has dialogue or quoted speech
- Says "today", "yesterday", "last week", "this morning" with what followed
- Tells you a thing that happened, not a thing they think

Phrases to look for: actual dialogue ("she said", "I told her", "he asked"), specific time markers, named people or places, sensory details.

## Structure

A Specific Story drops mid-scene. No setup, no "let me tell you about the time…", no "today I had an interesting experience". The first line is already inside the moment.

**Variant A — Tight scene (3-5 sentences)**

One moment, one beat of action, and the line that lands it. Often dialogue-driven.

```
[Drop into the scene — the most interesting beat first]

[1-2 lines of what happened — concrete actions, real words]

[The line that lands — a single observation or the punchline detail. Not a moral.]
```

**Variant B — Two-beat scene (5-8 sentences)**

A short setup, the moment that turns, and the resolution. Used when the story has a small arc and one beat isn't enough.

```
[Open in the middle of what's happening]

[The turn — what shifted, what was said, what someone did]

[The resolution — one beat, a line of dialogue, or the detail that closes it]
```

## Examples that work

**Tight scene:**

> Dad: *offers me a hand full of walnuts*
> 8 year old me: I can't have those, I'm allergic.
> Dad: What will happen?
> Me: They make my mouth itch.
> Dad: That's just what walnuts do.
> And that's when my dad learned that he's also allergic to walnuts.

> Stranger: Where are you from?
> Me: The Philippines
> Them: Your English is very good!
> Me: Thanks, people keep colonizing us

> My 6-year-old brother's teacher asked the class what's their favorite season and he said garlic powder.

> Asked my child what he was doing from the other room. I thought he said "taxes"… so I asked, "Taxes???" He walks in and says "Yup, taxes. I have been committing tax fraud for 500 years." What.

**Two-beat scene:**

> I told my landlord I'll be moving because I recently pivoted careers and have to cut my expenses. She called me and asked, "How much do you want to pay?" I gave her the price. Y'all… she took my rent down to the price I asked. Placed a concession on my account and said, "I really want you to stay, we love you."

> I told my therapist: "I feel worthless when I'm not productive." She said: "That's because capitalism taught you that your worth is your output. You are a human being, not a human doing." I've been staring at the ceiling for an hour.

> Missed the bottom step this morning and ate absolute shit. My kids, yelling from their bedrooms:
> 17: you good bruh -_-
> 16: first day with the new feet?
> 11 (from the bathroom): dude what fell??
> 6: MOM why would you fall like that!?

## Banned openings

The story has to open inside the moment. Setup framings kill the angle:

- "Let me tell you about the time…"
- "Today I had an interesting experience…"
- "So this happened…"
- "Funny story:"
- "Yesterday was wild because…"
- "I want to share something that happened…"
- "Story time:"

Drop the framing. Start with what happened or what was said.

## Banned closings

A Specific Story lands on a detail or a single observation. Lessons dilute it.

- "Just goes to show…"
- "The lesson here is…"
- "What this taught me:"
- "Moral of the story:"
- "Anyone else been through this?" (unless the question is genuinely specific and short)
- A summary of what just happened

If the story is good, the reader extracts their own meaning. Don't extract it for them.

## Anti-patterns

- **Story used as wrapper for a lesson.** The story is two sentences and the moral is four. This is not a Specific Story — it's a sermon with a prop. The scene must carry the post.
- **Vague details.** "A friend told me something interesting the other day…" — without names, time, or actual words, this is fiction. Reader can tell.
- **Composite scenes.** Mixing several real moments into one "representative" scene reads false. Keep one moment, even if smaller.
- **Cleaned-up dialogue.** Real people say "uh", trail off, contradict themselves. Sanitized dialogue ("I said: 'Indeed, I have considered this carefully'") sounds invented.
- **Too much exposition before the moment.** If three sentences pass before anything happens, the angle is wrong. The first line should already be in the scene.
- **Closing on tweet-bait.** "Drop a 🙌 if you've been there" — the scene was honest, then the close turns it into engagement farming. Pick one.

## Self-check before saving

Read the draft and ask: **does the first line drop me into the moment, or set it up?** If it sets up — cut the setup. The post should start where something is already happening.
