# Reframe

## What this angle is

A shift in thinking. The user used to see something one way, and now sees it differently. The post puts the *before* and the *after* side by side and lets the gap between them carry the weight.

The reader recognizes the shift in their own life ("yes, I went through the same change") or rejects it ("that's not why people change their mind") — both produce engagement. Reframe works because it shows thinking changing in real time, which is rare on social media where most posts arrive pre-decided.

## Signal in the dump

The dump contains a Reframe if the user:
- Describes a change in how they see something ("I used to think… now I think…")
- Names a moment when their understanding flipped
- Says they "finally understood" or "realized" something they'd been getting wrong
- Describes a first reaction vs a second reaction to the same thing
- Names something they used to defend that they no longer defend (or vice versa)

Phrases to look for: "used to think", "used to believe", "first reaction was X, second reaction was Y", "I finally understood", "turns out", "I realized that", "biggest mistake I made was", "for years I thought".

## Structure

Reframe needs both poles — before and after — and at least one beat showing the shift. Too short and the change reads as a slogan. Too long and the post turns into an essay.

**Variant A — Compressed two-pole (2-3 sentences)**

The before, the after, and one sharp line about the gap. No story.

```
[Before — what I used to think]

[After — what I think now, stated as a fact, not a journey]
```

**Variant B — Before / shift / after (3-5 sentences)**

The before, the moment or detail that changed it, and what changed. One specific beat in the middle is the difference between this and motivational fluff.

```
[Before — the old framing]

[The detail / moment / realization that flipped it]

[After — the new framing, with one consequence]
```

## Examples that work

**Compressed two-pole:**

> Called it "procrastination" for 30 years. Turns out it's executive dysfunction. Naming the enemy doesn't solve everything, but it stops the self-hatred.

> One of the weirdest parts of healing? Realizing you were actually the stable one the entire time. You were just reacting to unstable shit.

> I used to hate it when people said things like, "You're too sensitive," "You're too nice," or "You're so innocent." It always felt condescending. But then I realized: it's not an insult — it's a warning sign.

**Before / shift / after:**

> I saw a post from a dad that said he does a "family presentation night" once a month. Each kid picks a topic, practices it, presents it. His 8-year-old presented on dinosaurs. Eye contact. Q&A. The whole thing. My first reaction was that's extra. My second reaction was that kid is going to eat in job interviews.

> After 5 years of marriage I learned… we don't fight about dishes. We fight about feeling unheard. About carrying invisible load. About wanting partnership. The dishes are just the trigger.

> You don't magically become focused. You practice it. You notice you're distracted and snap out of it, over and over again until it's second nature. Most people have not trained this muscle, and they are not anywhere close to as focused as they think they are.

## Banned openings

These framings are overused and make the shift sound like a TED talk:

- "Here's what I learned…"
- "Lesson I wish I'd known sooner:"
- "The biggest lesson of my career:"
- "After 10 years of X, here's what I know now:"
- "PSA:"
- "Friendly reminder:"

State the *before* directly. The reader doesn't need to be told a lesson is coming.

## Banned closings

Reframe ends on the new framing. The new framing IS the point. Anything after that pads it out.

- "What changed your mind about X?"
- "Hope this helps someone."
- "Don't make the same mistake I did."
- A summary that restates both sides
- A call to action

If the reframe is real, it doesn't need a CTA — the reader is already thinking about their own version of it.

## Anti-patterns

- **Generic before, generic after.** "I used to think success was money, now I know it's freedom." This sounds like a refrigerator magnet. The before and after must be specific to this person's actual experience.
- **No real shift.** The post says "I used to think X, now I think Y" but X and Y aren't actually opposites. The reader can't see what changed. If you can't articulate the shift in one sentence, there's no shift.
- **The shift is the consensus.** Reframing toward common wisdom isn't a Reframe — it's just stating what everyone already says. The new framing should still surprise someone.
- **Too much story.** A Reframe is not a memoir. The story exists only to set up the contrast. If the story takes over, this is a Specific Story angle, not a Reframe.
- **Moralizing the shift.** Adding "and that's why we should all…" turns insight into a sermon. Drop the moral. Let the contrast do the work.

## Self-check before saving

Read the draft and ask: **can someone state, in one sentence, what specifically changed?** If they can't — the contrast isn't sharp enough. Rewrite the before and after so the gap between them is unmistakable.
