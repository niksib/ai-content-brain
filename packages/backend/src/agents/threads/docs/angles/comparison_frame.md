# Comparison Frame

## What this angle is

Two things placed side by side — eras, groups, ways of living, states, platforms, generations. The contrast carries the post. No long explanation. The reader fills in the meaning from the gap.

The reader's reaction is "yes, exactly this" or "you nailed why these two are different". Comparison Frame works because contrast compresses meaning — saying X = A and Y = B does more in two lines than a paragraph explaining either alone. The shape itself signals "look at the difference".

## Signal in the dump

The dump contains a Comparison Frame if the user:
- Compares two things — then vs now, this group vs that group, one approach vs another
- Names a contrast they've been thinking about
- Lists what's different between two states or eras
- Has a "X is for Y, but Z is for W" structure forming in their thinking

Phrases to look for: "X vs Y", "the difference between", "back then we…, now we…", "in [year] vs [year]", "[group A] does X. [group B] does Y."

## Structure

Comparison Frame requires symmetry. Both sides must be at the same scale, in the same form, and roughly the same length. Asymmetric comparisons feel sloppy.

**Variant A — Direct A/B (2-4 lines)**

Two parallel lines, one per side. Sometimes a third line that lands the implication.

```
[A is for X.]
[B is for Y.]

[Optional: one line that names what the contrast means.]
```

**Variant B — Three-tier comparison (3-5 lines)**

Three or more parallel items contrasted. Used when the comparison gains power from accumulation.

```
[A: claim about A]
[B: claim about B]
[C: claim about C]
```

**Variant C — Era contrast (3-6 lines)**

Two time periods compared — the same situation in different eras, showing how something shifted.

```
[Then — short, specific]

[Now — same situation, different version]
```

## Examples that work

**Direct A/B:**

> Instagram is for pretending you're fine.
> Threads is for saying, "I'm fucking tired," and realizing 400 people just exhaled with you.

> A $2,000 emergency fund means the car repair won't kill you.
> A $20,000 emergency fund means the job loss won't either.

**Three-tier:**

> Instagram is for pretending you're fine.
> LinkedIn is for pretending you have it together.
> Threads is where you admit you cried in the shower again.

**Era contrast:**

> Taking a sick day in:
> 2008 — "I'm taking a sick day." HR: Approved.
> 2026 — "Hello, I woke up feeling quite sick and was wondering if it's okay if I take the morning off to see the doctor. If I feel better, I will come in. If I don't, I will work remotely this afternoon. I will try to make up the hours…"

> 2003 might be the worst year to be born.
> 2008 — parents lose the house.
> 2013 — too young for bitcoin.
> 2020 — senior year on Zoom.
> 2021 — college in lockdown.
> 2025 — graduate into a frozen job market. actually cursed.

## Banned openings

The comparison should start with the comparison itself. Pre-framing it weakens the punch.

- "Have you noticed the difference between…"
- "Here's a comparison I've been thinking about:"
- "X vs Y — let's break it down."
- "It's interesting how X is so different from Y…"
- "Can we talk about how…"

State the first half of the comparison directly. The reader figures out it's a comparison the moment the second half lands.

## Banned closings

Comparison ends on the contrast or on one line of implication. Adding analysis dilutes it.

- "Which one are you?"
- "Drop a 1 if you're A, 2 if you're B"
- A long paragraph explaining why the contrast matters
- A pivot to "here's what we should do about it"
- Apologizing for the comparison ("not saying one is better, just observing")

The contrast lands or it doesn't. Defending it weakens it.

## Anti-patterns

- **Asymmetric comparison.** Side A is one short clean line. Side B is three sentences with qualifiers. The reader's eye registers the imbalance and the post feels off. Both sides should mirror each other in form.
- **False contrast.** "Instagram is hard. Threads is hard too." There's no actual contrast. Comparison Frame requires the two sides to actually differ in a meaningful way.
- **Stale comparison.** "Boomers vs Millennials", "iPhone vs Android" — overused contrasts have nothing left to give. The angle needs a fresh pairing or a fresh angle on a tired pairing.
- **Too many tiers.** Past 4-5 items in a multi-tier comparison, the structure starts to read as a list. Cut to the strongest contrasts.
- **Hidden moralizing.** Comparing two things while clearly favoring one ("People who do X are evolved. People who do Y are stuck.") turns the angle into Hot Take in disguise. If the user has a position, write a Hot Take. If they're naming a difference, keep both sides clean.
- **Comparison that needs explanation.** If the contrast doesn't land without a follow-up paragraph, the comparison itself isn't sharp enough. Rewrite both sides.

## Self-check before saving

Read the draft and ask: **do both sides hit at the same weight, in the same form?** If side A is sharper than side B, the post leans visibly. Either tighten side B or rewrite the contrast.
