# List of Specifics

## What this angle is

A short list — usually 3-7 items — where each item is a concrete, recognizable thing. Not a tutorial. Not steps to do anything. An inventory of small specific observations that together name something the reader has experienced but never seen written down.

The reader's reaction is "yes, every one of these", "you forgot one", or "wait, that one is so specific". Lists work because individual items are easy to scan, and a strong list creates the feeling of being seen — someone catalogued the small things you thought no one noticed.

## Signal in the dump

The dump contains a List of Specifics if the user:
- Enumerates things — what they love, hate, do, noticed, want, avoid, miss
- Catalogues small details that go together under one theme
- Lists what something *is*, not how to do it
- Names patterns made of multiple instances ("things that…", "ways people…", "signs of…")

Phrases to look for: "all the little things that…", "everything I…", "the kinds of…", commas stacking specifics, "and another thing".

## Structure

A list lives or dies on the specificity of each item. Generic items kill the angle no matter how many you stack.

**Variant A — Theme + bare list (5-10 lines)**

A single header line that names what's being listed, then the items. No explanation per item — the items are self-explanatory.

```
[Header line that names the category — short, specific]

[Item 1 — concrete, recognizable]
[Item 2]
[Item 3]
[…]
```

**Variant B — List with one-line riffs per item**

Each item gets a short follow-up line. Used when items need a beat of context to land. Riffs must stay short — usually under 10 words.

```
[Header line]

[Item 1]
[One-line riff that sharpens it]

[Item 2]
[One-line riff]

[…]
```

**Variant C — Implicit list (single paragraph, 3-5 specifics)**

The list is built into prose without bullets or numbers. Used when the list is short and rhythmic.

```
[A single paragraph that catalogues 3-5 specific things, separated by periods or commas]
```

## Examples that work

**Theme + bare list:**

> Luxuries that don't look like luxuries, but are:
> 1. Working from home 5 days a week
> 2. Taking a dump every morning
> 3. No addiction
> 4. Taking a shower
> 5. A safe home
> 6. Breathing through your nose
> 7. Having your mom and dad alive

> Sleep for 8h — not 6.
> Exercise for 1h — not 4.
> Learn for 1h — not 3.
> Read for 1h — not 2.
> Do deep work for 4h — not 8.
> You're a human. Not a machine.

> My executive function has three modes:
> 1. CEO of a Fortune 500
> 2. Intern on day one
> 3. Cat walking across keyboard

**List with riffs:**

> Tiny details that kill your appearance:
> 1. Crusty lips. People notice it more than you think. Dry = careless.
> 2. Dirty shoes. You could be well-dressed, but your shoes always tell the truth.
> 3. Untrimmed nose and ear hair. Instant ick. Easy fix, most just ignore it.

**Implicit list:**

> I don't want to be rich so I can buy a Rolex or a Chanel bag. I want to be rich so I can take my parents on holiday. So I can go to the gym randomly at 2 pm on a Wednesday. So I can cook every meal at home with fresh ingredients. So I can walk through nature in a place where I feel safe. So I can treat a friend to coffee without worrying if it tips me over budget.

## Banned openings

The list shouldn't be sold to the reader before it starts. Either drop straight into a header line, or start the implicit list directly.

- "Here's a list of…"
- "I made a list of…"
- "Top 5 things you need to know about…"
- "The ultimate guide to…"
- "X things every Y should know"
- "I've been thinking about all the…"

The header IS the opening. Don't introduce it.

## Banned closings

The list ends on the last item, or on one short summary line that lands the theme. Anything else flattens it.

- "Did I miss anything?"
- "Drop yours in the comments"
- "What's on your list?"
- "Save this for later"
- A long paragraph explaining the list

If the list earns a closing line, it should be a single sentence that sharpens the theme — not a question, not a CTA.

## Anti-patterns

- **Generic items.** "Be patient. Stay focused. Trust the process." This isn't a list, it's filler in list shape. Each item must be specific enough that only this person's life produced it.
- **Items that are actually instructions.** "1. Make your bed. 2. Drink water. 3. Exercise." This is a tutorial dressed as a list. List of Specifics is observation, not advice.
- **Mixed scales.** Item 1 is "lost my job", item 2 is "ran out of coffee". The items must sit at the same scale to feel like a real list.
- **Too many items.** Past 8-10, the reader skims and stops. Cut to the strongest 5-7.
- **Numbered when it should be flat.** Numbers imply ranking or sequence. Use them only when the order matters or when the count itself is part of the point.
- **One weak item drags the rest.** A list is only as strong as its weakest item. If item 4 is generic, the whole post softens. Cut it.

## Self-check before saving

Read each item alone and ask: **could this item have been written by anyone, or only by this person?** Any item that could have been written by anyone — cut it. The list is judged on its weakest entry, not its average.
